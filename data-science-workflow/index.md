## Introduction to the Data Science Workflow  

Data science is often presented as a collection of techniques — statistical tests, machine learning algorithms, deep learning architectures. Each is powerful in its own right, but technique alone does not produce good analysis. What separates reliable, impactful data science from ad hoc number-crunching is the process behind it: a disciplined, structured approach that connects a business question to a meaningful, well-evidenced answer.

The Data Science Workflow is that process. It describes the end-to-end journey from problem definition to recommendation — the stages every rigorous data science engagement moves through, regardless of the specific methods involved. Whether the task calls for a hypothesis test, a predictive model, or a deep learning architecture, the same fundamental questions apply: What are we trying to find out, and why? What data do we have, and is it fit for purpose? How should we model the problem, and how do we know if we have done it well? What do the results actually mean for the business?

It is important to emphasise that the workflow is iterative, not linear. In practice, insights uncovered during exploratory analysis frequently reshape the original problem definition. Data preparation often reveals quality issues that require revisiting the acquisition stage. Model evaluation can expose weaknesses that send the analyst back to feature engineering. This cycle of refinement is not a sign of poor planning — it is the nature of working with real data and real questions, and embracing it is a mark of mature analytical practice.

The sections that follow describe each stage of the workflow in detail, explaining both what happens at that stage and why it matters. Throughout, the projects in this portfolio are referenced to show how each stage is applied in practice — grounding the framework in concrete, worked examples rather than abstract description. ## 2. Problem Definition & Business Understanding  
Framing the right question before touching data. Connect this explicitly to your project scenarios — each of your projects opens with a business framing, which is worth highlighting here as a deliberate practice.
## 3. Data Acquisition & Initial Assessment  
Data sources (public datasets, synthetic data), understanding structure, variable types, and quality expectations. Reference Great Expectations here as your dedicated data quality project.
## 4. Exploratory Data Analysis (EDA)  
Distributions, correlations, outliers, class balance checks. This stage appears across virtually every project in your portfolio and is worth signposting as a consistent practice.
## 5. Data Preparation & Feature Engineering  
Cleaning, encoding, scaling, handling imbalance, transformation. You have excellent concrete examples here — the class imbalance fix in KNN, the feature-engineered logistic regression model, and scaling differences across SVM vs tree-based models.
## 6. Model Selection — Choosing the Right Approach  
This is a high-value section that demonstrates professional judgement. Cover the decision logic: when to use statistical testing vs ML vs deep learning; parametric vs non-parametric methods; interpretability vs performance trade-offs. Your portfolio is diverse enough to make this genuinely substantive.
## 7. The Machine Learning Workflow (Sub-Section)  
Within model selection and training, go deeper on the ML-specific pipeline: train/test splits, cross-validation, hyperparameter tuning, evaluation metrics (accuracy, F1, AUC-ROC), and overfitting management. Reference your supervised ML projects here — Decision Trees through SVM, with benchmark comparisons (your DT/RF/GBT accuracy progression is a perfect illustration).
## 8. Model Evaluation & Interpretability  
Metrics, confusion matrices, and — critically — why a high-accuracy model that can't be explained has limited business value. Your SHAP page is a natural anchor here, and it ties back to the GBT model, which creates a nice cross-portfolio narrative.
## 9. Communicating Results & Business Recommendations  
Translating outputs into decisions. Emphasise that conclusions and next steps in each project are deliberate — findings are always framed in terms of the business question, not just model performance.

A summary table at the bottom mapping each project to the primary workflow stages it demonstrates (EDA, Feature Engineering, Modelling, Evaluation, Interpretability, etc.).
